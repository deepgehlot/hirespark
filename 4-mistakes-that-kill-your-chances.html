The 4 mistakes I see every day that kill your chances

After rebuilding 500+ resumes, there’s one thing I can say with my full chest: Most people’s resumes undersell them so badly it’s almost painful to watch. Not because they’re bad, unskilled, or unqualified but because they make the same mistakes over and over again.

So here’s the version people actually understand, without the corporate bullshit:

1. Writing like you’re scared to take credit

People send me resumes saying things like:

  • “Helped customers when needed.”
  • “Assisted the team.”
  • “Supported daily operations.”

Then I ask a few quick questions (email, nothing dramatic), and suddenly it turns out:

  • They were the one everyone relied on when things got busy.
  • They trained every new hire because “you explain things better.”
  • They handled every angry customer because no one else wanted to.

But on paper? They make themselves sound like a benchwarmer. Recruiters can’t magically guess your impact. You have to actually show it.

2. Copying your job description instead of telling your story

This is the #1 resume killer. People paste bullets like:

  • “Managed cash register.”
  • “Greeted customers.”
  • “Handled administrative tasks.”

Okay. That’s what the job IS, not what YOU did with it. If your bullets look like they came from the job posting, you’re done.

Example:
❌ “Handled inventory.”
✅ “Reduced inventory errors by reorganizing stock system, making it easier for team to find and restock items.”

Same job completely different impact.

3. Writing paragraphs instead of clean bullets

If your resume looks like a wall of text, it doesn’t matter how good you are no one is reading that. Recruiters skim like they’re speed-running. ATS systems skim. Humans skim.

Paragraphs bury your achievements. Bullets highlight them. Your resume should read like: quick hits, clear wins, fast information.

4. Using vague language that sounds like you’re talking without saying anything

Bullets like: “Responsible for…”, “Worked on…”, “Ensured…” are empty calories. They don’t tell the recruiter anything.

Use strong, human verbs: improved, redesigned, led, solved, trained, introduced, streamlined. You don’t need to exaggerate just be specific.

Example:
❌ “Ensured customer satisfaction.”
✅ “Resolved 20+ customer issues per shift with a calm, solutions-focused approach that consistently prevented escalations.”

Completely different energy.

The truth nobody tells you: Most people aren’t underqualified. They’re underrepresented on paper.
